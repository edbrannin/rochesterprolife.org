Healthier Babies

 

When you are pregnant, you share your body with your unborn child. Everything that you eat or drink or inhale, you also give to your baby. So a simple rule is, if you wouldnât give it to baby after she is born, do not give it to her before she is born. And if you would want baby to enjoy fresh air, exercise, and rest, then you need fresh air, exercise, and rest now.

 

For some of us, this means changing how we lived before we got pregnant, and some of those changes are not easy. Here is some information about why they are important and how you can get help to do what is best for you AND baby.

 

Smoking

 

Smoking during pregnancy is the most important cause of poor infant health and even death. When a pregnant woman smokes (or even inhales second hand smoke when others smoke around her), she cuts off the full flow of oxygen to her unborn baby. The babyâs lungs do not develop properly and the baby may be born early, before he is fully developed. The damage done to babies in the womb lasts for a lifetime.

 

Quitting smoking is not easy, but it can be done, and what better reason than to keep your baby healthy? Check out these Tips for Quitting. To get help, call 1-866-697-8487, or contact PNMC for a FREE workbook that will help you create a plan and stick with it. GRATCC (Greater Rochester Area Tobacco Cessation Center) has cessation support tools for teens and others as well as information for providers. The New York State Quit Site has links to tools to quit, application for free NRT, and education for providers with CME credit.

 

Alcohol

 

Alcohol causes birth defects. We used to think that drinking moderately during pregnancy was OK. But the more researchers learn, the more we understand that no level of alcohol can be considered entirely safe. So even though you may know women who drank during pregnancy and had healthy babies, it is a risk that has lifelong consequences. For more information, go to http://www.nofas.org/family/

 

Street Drugs

 

Street drugs also damage the unborn child, sometimes causing emotional as well as physical problems. If you have been using drugs, now may be the best time to get help. Here are some programs that can help you deal with addictions.

 

Dental Care

 

Yes, a womanâs dental health can affect her baby! Women with serious periodontal disease may be seven times more likely to deliver a baby that is too early and too small. And being pregnant can affect a womanâs teeth and gums. You may have heard the old wivesâ tale about losing a tooth with each pregnancyâ¦ well, it is not entirely false. But it is preventable. Taking care of your teeth and gums is doubly important during pregnancy (for your health and for babyâs health). So it is important that pregnant women see a dentist early to identify and treat any tooth or gum disease.

 

And after baby is born, remember not to put things in your babyâs mouth that have been in your mouth â the bacteria that cause cavities can be transferred to your baby and cause disease even before baby has teeth! And that early damage affects the permanent teeth, not just the baby teeth.
